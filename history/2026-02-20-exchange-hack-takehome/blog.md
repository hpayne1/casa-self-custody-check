# Blog: After an exchange hack: first-aid now, a custody plan next (and why both matter)

When an exchange hack hits the timeline, it creates two different experiences at once. Some people may be impacted (or unsure whether they are). Others aren't directly affected, but feel that familiar unease: "If my assets live behind someone else's login, what do I actually control?"

If you're feeling stressed, that reaction is rational. The goal in moments like this isn't to move fast. It's to stay safe, avoid scams, and then build a custody posture that matches what you're trying to do long term.

## The first hour: avoid second-order losses

After incidents, scammers reliably show up. They impersonate support, share fake links, and exploit urgency.

If you might be impacted:
- Verify updates only through official channels.
- Ignore DMs and "support" accounts contacting you first.
- Don't follow unfamiliar "recovery" instructions under stress.

Your priority is protecting yourself while facts settle.

## The bigger issue: custody model, not just headlines

Exchanges are popular because they're convenient. But convenience can hide a structural risk: [counterparty risk](https://blog.casa.io/exchanges-vs-self-custody-risks/).

A simple mental model:
- **Custodial account**: you have access to a balance, but the platform controls withdrawals/keys. In an [exchange failure scenario](https://blog.casa.io/exchange-failure/), access can change quickly—and sometimes permanently.
- **Self-custody**: you control the keys. Ownership doesn't depend on a third party authorizing withdrawals.

This is not a moral judgment, and it's not a demand that everyone becomes an expert overnight. It's a prompt to match your custody approach to your time horizon.

## A calm path to self-custody (without overreacting)

Most people do best with a gradual approach.

### 1) Separate "spend" from "long-term."

Decide what you need for near-term activity versus what you're holding for months/years. Long-term bitcoin (and other long-term holdings) is where reducing counterparty risk matters most.

### 2) Start small.

Make a small transfer to a self-custody setup first. Get comfortable receiving and sending before moving meaningful amounts.

### 3) Test recovery before you scale.

If you can't reliably recover, you don't truly control the asset. Backups, recovery practice, and clear documentation matter.

### 4) Reduce single points of failure as you grow.

As amounts grow, it's reasonable to consider multi-key approaches (often called [multisig](https://blog.casa.io/an-introduction-to-multisig/)) that reduce the chance one lost device—or one compromised key—creates a catastrophic outcome. If you're evaluating multi-key systems, it's worth understanding how "mobile keys" fit into security models (see: [Mobile Key overview](https://blog.casa.io/casa-keymaster-security-mobile-key-overview/)).

### 5) Operators/custodians: treat custody like internal controls.

If you custody assets for others or manage a treasury, the right posture is familiar: separation of duties, multi-party approvals, and auditable process. These are risk-reduction tools, not guarantees—but they meaningfully change failure modes.

## A steady close

If today's news changed how you think about custody, that's useful. Don't rush. Take one safe step now, then build a plan you can maintain over time. Durable security is usually the product of calm habits—not a single reaction.
